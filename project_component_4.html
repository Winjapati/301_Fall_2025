Final Project Presentation Instructions

Schedule & Timing

  • Presentations will take place over the last two class days.
  • Talk length: 7 minutes of presenting + 1 minute for questions.
  • Be prepared to present on either day in case we need to shift the schedule.

Project Website (Instead of Slides)

Instead of a slide deck, you will build a small project website and present it to the class.
This site will be hosted on your GitHub Pages site (username.github.io).

Basic Requirements

  • Your project must be presented as a website (HTML/CSS; Quarto optional).
  • The site must be publicly accessible at your GitHub Pages URL.
  • The project must contain at least three clearly separated sections:
    • Dataset
    • Methods
    • Results & Interpretation

Suggested Structure

You can structure this as multiple pages or as a single page with good navigation.

1. Home / Overview

  • Project title and your name.
  • Short summary of your research question or goal.
  • Outline of the site (what’s on each page/section).

2. Dataset

  • Which text(s) you chose and why.
  • Language(s), source(s), and approximate size (words, chapters, sections).
  • How you cleaned the data (tokenization, normalization, encoding issues).
  • Any problems you ran into and how you handled them.

3. Methods

  • What you did in Python, in plain language.
  • Examples: token and type counts, TTR, frequency lists, POS tagging, lemmatization, dependencies.
  • spaCy or WordNet analyses you used.
  • Any custom function(s) you wrote and what they were for.
  • You can include small code snippets, but focus on what the code does.
  • Discuss any outside help that you received, such as from an LLM, Stack Overflow, or a friend.

4. Results & Interpretation

  • At least one visualization embedded in the page.
  • Key numerical results (counts, ratios, patterns, comparisons).
  • Clear explanation of what the results mean, not just what they are.
  • Discussion of why you think these patterns appear:
    • language properties
    • translation choices
    • stylistic differences
  • Short “What I’d do next” section.

Presenting Your Website

  • During your 7-minute talk, you will navigate your website on the screen.
  • Use it to guide your story:
    • Overview → Dataset → Methods → Results & Interpretation.
  • Don’t read the site word-for-word!

Design Expectations

  • The site does not need to be fancy, but it must be:
    • Readable (good contrast, clear font sizes).
    • Organized (headings, sections).
    • Easy to navigate (links or a simple nav bar).
  • Basic CSS is encouraged.
  • You should avoid huge walls of text.

Delivery Expectations

  • Stay within the time limit: 7 minutes + 2 minutes for questions.
  • Speak clearly and at a normal pace.
  • Assume your audience knows the course tools but not your project.
  • Focus on reasoning, not just procedure.

What Will Be Graded

  • Clarity: How clearly you explain your project and guide us through the site.
  • Technical understanding: You understand what your code and metrics do.
  • Quality of analysis: Thoughtfulness of results and interpretation.
  • Website quality: Readability, organization, navigation, design.
  • Professionalism: Timing, preparation, and delivery.

What to Submit

  • Before talks are held (final week of class), submit to Canvas:
    • a link to your live GitHub Pages project site
      (e.g., https://username.github.io/project-name/).
    • a link to your GitHub repository containing all project files.
