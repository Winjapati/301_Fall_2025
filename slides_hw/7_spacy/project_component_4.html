Final Project Presentation Instructions

Schedule & Timing

  • Presentations will take place over the last two class days.
  • Talk length: 7 minutes of presenting + 2 minutes for questions.
  • Total time per person: 9 minutes (hard cutoff).
  • Be prepared to present on either day in case we need to shift the schedule.

Project Website (Instead of Slides)

Instead of a slide deck, you will build a small project website and present it to the class. This site will be hosted on your GitHub Pages (your username.github.io site).

Basic Requirements

  • Your project must be presented as a website (HTML/CSS, optionally Quarto).
  • The site must be publicly accessible at your GitHub Pages URL.
  • The project must have at least 3 distinct sections (can be separate pages or clearly separated sections on one page):
    • Dataset
    • Methods
    • Results & Interpretation

Suggested Structure

You can implement this as multiple pages (e.g., index.html, methods.html, etc.) or as sections on a single page with navigation links.

  1. Home / Overview (e.g., index.html)
    • Project title and your name.
    • Short summary of your research question or goal.
    • Brief outline of the site (what’s on each page/section).
  2. Dataset
    • Which text(s) you chose and why.
    • Language(s), source(s), and approximate size (words, chapters, sections, etc.).
    • How you cleaned the data (tokenization, normalization, encoding issues, etc.).
    • Any problems you ran into and how you handled them.
  3. Methods
    • What you did in Python, described in plain language.
    • Examples: token and type counts, TTR, frequency lists, POS tagging, lemmatization, dependencies.
    • Any spaCy or WordNet analyses you used.
    • Any custom function(s) you wrote and what they were for.
    • You can include short, focused code snippets, but the emphasis should be on what the code does.
  4. Results & Interpretation
    • At least one visualization (e.g., bar chart, line chart, scatter plot) embedded as an image or HTML output.
    • Key numerical results (counts, ratios, patterns, comparisons).
    • Clear explanation of what the results mean, not just what they are.
    • Discussion of why you think these patterns appear (language properties, translation choices, stylistic differences, etc.).
    • Short “What I’d do next” section: follow-up analyses or questions you’d pursue with more time.

Presenting Your Website

  • During your 7-minute talk, you will navigate your own website on the projector.
  • Use your site to guide the story:
    • Start on the overview/home section.
    • Move to Dataset → Methods → Results & Interpretation.
  • Do not read the site word-for-word. Use it as a visual outline.
  • Keep your explanation focused: Data → Methods → Results → Interpretation.

Design Expectations

  • The site does not need to be fancy, but it should be:
    • Readable (good contrast, reasonable font sizes).
    • Organized (headings, sections, logical flow).
    • Easy to navigate (menu or links to sections/pages).
  • Basic CSS is encouraged (use your existing course site styles if you like).
  • Avoid huge walls of text; break content into paragraphs and bullet points.

Delivery Expectations

  • Stay within the time limit: 7 minutes of talking, 2 minutes for questions.
  • Speak clearly and at a reasonable pace.
  • Assume your audience knows the tools (Python, spaCy, etc.) but not your project.
  • Focus on explaining your choices and your reasoning, not just listing steps.

What Will Be Graded

  • Clarity: How clearly you explain your project and guide us through the site.
  • Technical understanding: How well you understand what your code and metrics actually do.
  • Quality of analysis: Depth and thoughtfulness of your results and interpretation.
  • Website quality: Organization, readability, navigation, and basic design.
  • Professionalism: Timing, preparation, and overall delivery.

What to Submit

  • A link to your live GitHub Pages project site (e.g., https://username.github.io/project-name/ or a clearly labeled subpage of your main site).
  • A link to your GitHub repository containing the project website files (HTML/CSS, images, etc.).
  • Submit both links before class on the day of your presentation.
